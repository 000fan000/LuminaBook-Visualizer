If I were a serious reader of philosophy, political theory, sociology, history, and other dense nonfiction, I would care much less about "turning pages beautifully" and much more about *thinking with the text*. The ideal reader would feel halfway between a book reader, a research notebook, and a knowledge-management system.

Here are the 10 functions I would most want, ranked roughly by importance.

### 1. Atomic Highlighting & Annotation

Not just highlighting passages.

I want to:

* Highlight individual claims, arguments, definitions, examples, and objections.
* Tag them differently.
* Attach notes at paragraph, sentence, or phrase level.
* Link multiple highlights together.

Example:

> Foucault's definition of power

and

> Gramsci's concept of hegemony

can be connected directly.

Most readers treat highlights as colored paint. I want them treated as knowledge objects.

---

### 2. Bidirectional Linking Between Books

A reader should let me create links across books.

Example:

While reading Discipline and Punish, I link a passage to a section in Seeing Like a State.

Later:

* Open either passage.
* See the relationship.
* Navigate both directions.

This is how scholars actually read.

---

### 3. Automatic Concept Extraction

The reader identifies recurring concepts.

For example:

* bureaucracy
* sovereignty
* alienation
* rationality
* legitimacy

Across hundreds of books.

Then I can click "legitimacy" and see:

* all occurrences
* my highlights
* related notes
* related books

Essentially a personal intellectual index.

---

### 4. Argument Mapping

This is probably the biggest missing feature in today's readers.

Instead of notes only, I want:

```
Main Thesis
 ├─ Argument A
 │   ├─ Evidence
 │   └─ Assumption
 ├─ Argument B
 └─ Counterargument
```

A philosophy reader should help reconstruct the structure of reasoning.

When reading A Theory of Justice or Being and Time, understanding the architecture matters more than remembering quotations.

---

### 5. Progressive Reading History

I want to know:

* When did I first encounter this idea?
* How did my interpretation change?

Example:

```
2022: "Power = domination"
2024: "Power is productive"
2026: "Power is relational"
```

The reader becomes an intellectual diary.

---

### 6. AI That Understands the Book Context

Not generic chatbot features.

Instead:

Ask:

> Explain this paragraph using only concepts already introduced in the book.

Or:

> What assumptions does this chapter make?

Or:

> Compare this argument to Weber.

The AI should know:

* book context
* previous chapters
* my notes

Most current AI readers answer in isolation.

---

### 7. Reading Graph / Knowledge Graph

Visualize:

```
Foucault
   ↔ Power
   ↔ Discipline
   ↔ Prison

Weber
   ↔ Bureaucracy
   ↔ Rationalization

Arendt
   ↔ Authority
   ↔ Totalitarianism
```

Generated from:

* books
* annotations
* links
* notes

This becomes a map of one's intellectual world.

---

### 8. Citation & Export Workflow

A serious reader eventually writes.

I want one-click export to:

* Markdown
* Obsidian
* Roam
* Logseq
* Zotero
* academic citation formats

Every highlight should preserve:

* page number
* edition
* location
* bibliographic metadata

No manual cleanup.

---

### 9. Comparative Reading Mode

Open two or more texts side by side.

Examples:

* The Republic vs. Politics
* The Communist Manifesto vs. The Road to Serfdom

The reader should automatically surface:

* shared concepts
* disagreements
* references
* overlapping themes

This would save enormous amounts of scholarly effort.

---

### 10. "Return to Important Ideas" System

Most readers optimize for finishing books.

Serious readers optimize for retaining ideas.

The app should periodically surface:

* forgotten highlights
* unresolved notes
* recurring concepts
* contradictions

Example:

> You've highlighted "bureaucracy" in 17 books. Would you like a synthesis?

This transforms reading from consumption into accumulation of understanding.

---

## Features I Would Explicitly Not Prioritize

Many reader apps focus on:

* page-turn animations
* realistic book aesthetics
* social feeds
* reading streaks
* reading speed metrics
* gamification

For philosophy and social science readers, these are low value.

The real bottleneck is not reading text.

The bottleneck is:

1. Understanding arguments.
2. Connecting ideas across books.
3. Retaining concepts over years.
4. Turning reading into original thought.

A reader optimized for deep nonfiction would therefore look less like Kindle and more like a hybrid of:

* [Obsidian](https://obsidian.md?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
* [Zotero](https://www.zotero.org?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
* [Roam Research](https://roamresearch.com?utm_source=chatgpt.com)
* a scholarly annotation tool
* and an AI research assistant

all embedded directly into the reading experience.
